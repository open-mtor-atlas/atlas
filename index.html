One switch, two faces — 25 years of mTOR

mTOR is the protein that decides whether your cells build or clean house. Turn it up and you get growth, muscle, and immune defense. Turn it down and you get autophagy — the recycling program linked to a longer, healthier life in every organism it's been tested in. This Atlas tracks the evidence behind both faces, one PubMed-verified study at a time.

How we got here

Every entry in this Atlas traces back to a real paper. Here are six moments that built the field — from a soil sample on a Pacific island to a clinical trial that didn't work.
1975
A molecule from Rapa Nui
Vézina and colleagues isolate a compound from Streptomyces bacteria in a soil sample from Rapa Nui (Easter Island), with antifungal activity nobody can yet explain. They name it after the island: rapamycin. DOI: 10.7164/antibiotics.28.721
1994
The target gets a name
Sabatini and colleagues identify the protein rapamycin actually binds inside human cells — the Mammalian Target Of Rapamycin. Two decades of "what does this drug even do?" finally has a molecular answer. DOI: 10.1016/0092-8674(94)90570-3
2002–2004
One protein, two complexes
Kim et al. discover Raptor, the partner protein that defines mTOR Complex 1. Two years later, Sarbassov et al. discover Rictor — defining a second, rapamycin-resistant complex, mTORC2. mTOR turns out to have two faces at the molecular level too. DOI: 10.1016/s0092-8674(02)00808-5 · DOI: 10.1016/j.cub.2004.06.054
2009
The first drug shown to extend mammalian lifespan
The NIA Interventions Testing Program, led by Harrison and colleagues, shows rapamycin extends lifespan in genetically heterogeneous mice — even when treatment starts late in life. First pharmacological intervention ever shown to do this in a mammal. DOI: 10.1038/nature08221
2018
From mice to people
Mannick and colleagues show low-dose mTOR inhibition reduces infection rates and improves vaccine response in elderly volunteers — the first hint the mouse result might translate to humans. DOI: 10.1126/scitranslmed.aaq1564
2021
The reality check
The follow-up phase 3 trial — 1,024 participants — misses its primary endpoint. The drug still switched on the right antiviral genes; it just didn't reduce clinically symptomatic illness. This Atlas keeps that result next to the success that came before it, because a database that only remembers the wins isn't science. DOI: 10.1016/S2666-7568(21)00062-3

Two Axes of Evidence

A study's evidence tier (how strong the proof is) and its study type (what kind of experiment produced it) are two separate things — a mouse study can be extremely well-designed and still cap out at Tier C, simply because it isn't a human. This matrix cross-references both, so you can see, for example, exactly how many of the Atlas's studies are human trials that also carry Tier B evidence.

What this section is

A pathway map or a study table can only show what's already known. This section is the opposite: real gaps that show up once you actually connect the Atlas's own entities and studies — questions a landmark paper raised but didn't answer, or two findings that don't yet fit together cleanly. Each one links back to the specific entities and studies behind it, so you can go check the primary literature yourself rather than take the gap on faith.

Q1Open
"Can mTORC1 be selectively inhibited without disrupting mTORC2?"
Rapamycin was built as an mTORC1 drug, but with chronic dosing it also breaks mTORC2 — that's what causes the insulin resistance seen in mice. Nobody has shown a way to hit mTORC1 without eventually catching mTORC2 too.
mTORC1mTORC2RapamycinLAM2012
Q2Partially addressed
"What is the optimal pulsatile (on/off) dosing schedule for mTOR inhibition?"
One study found that just 3 months of rapamycin in middle-aged mice extended lifespan afterward — the benefit outlasted the drug. That suggests dosing doesn't need to be continuous, but nobody has mapped out the actual optimal on/off schedule.
RapamycinBIT2016
Q3Open
"Why hasn't rapamycin's mouse lifespan benefit shown up yet in a completed human trial?"
The PEARL trial — the first completed long-term human RCT of rapamycin for aging — found no significant change on its primary endpoint. That could mean the effect doesn't translate to humans, or it could mean the dose, duration, or endpoint chosen weren't the right ones to detect it. The Atlas doesn't have enough data yet to tell which.

Q4Open
"Do leucine, isoleucine, and valine act on mTORC1 through genuinely separate mechanisms?"
Restricting isoleucine or valine (but not leucine) improved metabolic health in one Alzheimer's mouse study, even though all three are grouped together as "BCAAs." That result implies they're not interchangeable — but it's one study, and it doesn't yet explain the mechanism behind the difference.

Creator & Curator — Prague, Czech Republic — Age 15

Oliver is a high school student in Prague with a self-directed research interest in mTOR signaling, longevity biology, and evidence-based science curation. He started the Open mTOR Atlas to build the kind of resource he wished existed when he began reading primary literature on the pathway: one structured database that holds studies, mechanisms, interventions, and honest evidence grades side by side, rather than scattered across dozens of review articles and pathway maps.

His current focus is mTOR regulation under dynamic rather than steady-state conditions — most of the literature (including most of the studies in this Atlas) measures mTORC1/mTORC2 activity as if it were a fixed dial, set once by a drug or a diet and left there. In a real cell, activity actually oscillates in pulses tied to feeding cycles, growth factor bursts, and circadian rhythm. Oliver is interested in whether the pattern of activation — not just its average level — is what actually determines outcomes like autophagy or growth, which is also the open question behind "Q2" in this Atlas's Open Questions tab.

What this project is

The Open mTOR Atlas is a structured, peer-reviewed-literature-grounded database of the mTOR signaling pathway. It currently holds 66 cross-linked entities and 100 studies, spanning: the pathway's upstream regulators (PI3K, Akt, TSC1/TSC2, Rheb, the Rag GTPases, Sestrin2, AMPK, and the growth hormone/IGF-1 axis further upstream still); its two core complexes (mTORC1, mTORC2) and their direct downstream effectors (S6K1, ULK1, Atg5); the drugs, supplements, and interventions that act on it (rapamycin, everolimus, temsirolimus, metformin, acarbose, caloric restriction, macronutrient ratio, leucine/BCAA restriction — alongside supplements like resveratrol and curcumin that were tested and found not to work); and the outcomes and disease contexts those interventions touch — longevity, autophagy, muscle growth, immune function, kidney transplant rejection, renal cell carcinoma, Huntington's disease, Alzheimer's disease — plus their side effects, like the insulin resistance that comes with chronic rapamycin dosing. Every entry traces back to a specific paper, indexed with its evidence tier, model organism, and DOI.

It deliberately includes negative results — studies where a popular longevity compound did not extend lifespan — because a database that only shows positive findings misrepresents the actual state of the science. The Interventions Testing Program's published null results for resveratrol, curcumin, and simvastatin sit in the Atlas with the same visibility as the positive rapamycin findings.

What makes it different

Pathway databases like Reactome or KEGG map the biochemistry. PubMed lets you search papers one at a time. Neither one lets you ask "what's the actual evidence, at what tier, that rapamycin extends lifespan in humans and not just mice?" and get a direct answer with the receipts attached. That's the gap the Atlas is built to close — starting narrow, with mTOR, and built by someone still early enough in their scientific training to insist that every claim traces back to a real paper.

How it's built and kept current

Every study enters the Atlas through the same four-step pipeline:

1. Source
Candidate papers are found via PubMed, prioritizing landmark discovery papers, systematic reviews, and large human RCTs over secondary commentary.
2. Verify
Each citation's PMID, DOI, year, and journal are confirmed against PubMed metadata before the entry is written — no citation is added from memory alone.
3. Grade
Every study gets an evidence tier (A = systematic review of human data, down through animal, invertebrate, and mechanistic-only) based on the strength of the model system, not the size of the headline finding.
4. Link
Entities mentioned in the study (genes, drugs, outcomes) are connected to it in the graph, so the same paper surfaces wherever any of its subjects is explored.

Every entity and study in the Atlas is sourced from primary, checkable resources — never from secondary write-ups or memory.

PubMed
The NIH's biomedical literature index. Primary source for study metadata — title, authors, journal, date, and DOI are pulled and verified against PubMed's own record before an entry is written.
bioRxiv
bioRxiv
Source for preprints — studies not yet peer-reviewed. These are explicitly flagged as preprints, never treated as equivalent to published work.
ClinicalTrials.gov
ClinicalTrials.gov
U.S. registry of clinical trials. Lets the Atlas show ongoing human studies and what they're testing, even before results exist.
Publisher DOIs
Every DOI resolves to the publisher's own page — Nature, Cell, Science, NEJM, Lancet, and others — so any claim traces back to the original paper in one click.
Studies were selected to cover the pathway end-to-end: the landmark discovery papers that defined mTOR biology, the strongest available human evidence (systematic reviews and RCTs), representative animal and mechanistic work for each key node, and — deliberately — negative and null results, so the Atlas reflects what the field has actually shown rather than only its successes.
